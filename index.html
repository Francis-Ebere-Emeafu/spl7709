Microeconomics Concept · Scarcity & Opportunity Cost
Meet Ramesh — The Common Man's Dilemma
🎬
Opening Scene — Ramesh at the Petrol Pump, Pune
EFE
EMEAFU

Picture Ramesh, a delivery driver in Pune, filling his petrol scooter every three days. At ₹103 per litre and 35 km to the litre, he spends nearly ₹4,500 a month just on fuel. That's 22% of his income — gone before groceries.

CFE
CHIOMA

In economics, we call this an opportunity cost. Every rupee Ramesh spends on petrol is a rupee he cannot spend on his child's school fees or his wife's medical visit. Scarcity of income makes every choice painful.

EFE
EMEAFU

Now multiply Ramesh by 280 million two-wheelers on Indian roads. That is the scale of the fuel dependence problem — and the scale of the opportunity for electric vehicles.

Economics Concept: Opportunity Cost
Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative foregone when a choice is made. For Ramesh, spending ₹4,500/month on petrol means forgoing education, health, or savings. Policy that reduces his fuel bill effectively raises his real income without changing his wage.

Microeconomics Concept · Demand, Price Elasticity & Subsidies
Creating Demand — How the Government Nudged the Market
🛵
The Price Gap — Petrol Scooter vs Electric Scooter Before & After Subsidy
CFE
CHIOMA

In 2019, a good electric scooter cost ₹1.2 lakh. A comparable petrol scooter: ₹65,000. For Ramesh, the price differential alone killed demand before a test ride even happened.

EFE
EMEAFU

Enter the government's FAME II scheme — ₹11,500 crore — which directly subsidised buyers at point of sale, lowering the price Ramesh actually paid. Each rupee of subsidy triggered a market multiplier of 9 to 21 times in actual sales. Extraordinary policy efficiency.

CFE
CHIOMA

This is a demand-side subsidy — it shifts the demand curve right. But when subsidies were cut under EMPS 2024, sales slowed. That tells us EV demand is still price-elastic — the market hasn't reached self-sustaining tipping point yet.

📈
Demand Curve Shift — Effect of FAME II Subsidy on E-2W Market
Economics Concept: Price Elasticity of Demand
Price elasticity measures how much quantity demanded changes when price changes. EVs in India remain elastic — a 15% subsidy cut under EMPS 2024 caused measurable sales slowdown. As EV prices fall toward petrol-parity, demand will become inelastic. India is currently in the elastic transition phase.

Microeconomics Concept · Supply-Side Incentives, Economies of Scale & GST Tax Wedge
Building Supply — Making India the Factory Floor
🏭
Supply-Side Story — PLI Scheme, Battery Manufacturing & GST Tax Wedge
EFE
EMEAFU

The PLI scheme — ₹25,938 crore — subsidises domestic EV manufacturing, shifting the supply curve right. More EVs produced at every price level. As output scales, firms benefit from economies of scale — the average cost per unit falls with volume.

CFE
CHIOMA

Battery costs globally have dropped over 90% in a decade — from $1,200 per kWh in 2010 to under $100 today. That is pure economies of scale at work. India's ACC Battery Scheme targets 50 GWh of domestic cell production so we capture this learning curve at home.

EFE
EMEAFU

And the GST tax wedge is genius quiet policy: EVs taxed at just 5%, petrol cars at 28% plus cess. The government tilts relative prices without writing a single cheque — making the market itself do the heavy lifting.

Microeconomics Concept · Market Structure — Oligopoly, Competition, Market Power
From One Player to Many — The Market Restructures
🏢
Market Structure Evolution — Dominant Firm (2022) → Oligopoly (2025)
CFE
CHIOMA

In 2022, Ola Electric controlled 35% of the e-2W market — classic dominant firm behaviour. It could price aggressively, absorb losses with VC funding, and crowd out rivals. Consumers had limited choice and paid a premium.

EFE
EMEAFU

By 2025: TVS leads at 23.5%, Bajaj at 19%, Ather at 16%, Ola down to 15% — a 51% sales collapse driven by service failures and competition. Legacy manufacturers with dealer networks and service centres won the long game.

CFE
CHIOMA

This is oligopoly theory at work. Competition between four near-equal firms forces prices toward marginal cost. Ola launched a scooter at ₹74,000 — the market punishes monopoly rents and rewards the consumer.

Economics Concept: Oligopoly vs. Perfect Competition
An oligopoly is a market with a few large firms with strategic interdependence. India's EV market has evolved from near-monopoly toward genuine rivalry, delivering lower prices and more choice. Tata Motors' car market share fell from 61% to 39% in one year as Mahindra and MG entered aggressively — classic competitive market dynamics.

Microeconomics Concept · TCO, Externalities, Market Failure & Pigouvian Tax
The Economics of Switching — Externalities & the True Cost
🌫️
Negative Externalities — The Costs Society Pays That Ramesh Doesn't
📐
Market Failure Diagram — Social Cost vs Private Cost of Petrol Vehicles
CFE
CHIOMA

Ramesh never pays for the pollution his scooter creates. Those health costs, carbon costs, and oil import costs are negative externalities — costs borne by society, not by the individual buyer. This gap between private cost and social cost is a classic market failure.

EFE
EMEAFU

EV subsidies and high GST on petrol cars are Pigouvian corrections — named after economist Arthur Pigou. They force private prices to reflect social costs. In Delhi, where vehicles cause over 25% of particulate pollution, mass EV adoption could prevent thousands of respiratory illnesses annually.

Economics Concept: Negative Externalities & Market Failure
A negative externality arises when a transaction imposes costs on uninvolved third parties. The socially optimal output of petrol vehicles is lower than the market equilibrium. EV subsidies, high GST on ICE vehicles, and fuel taxes are Pigouvian corrections designed to align private incentives with social welfare — closing the gap between private and social cost.

Microeconomics Concept · Coordination Failure, Network Effects, Barriers to Entry
The Chicken-and-Egg Problem — Why Chargers Matter
⚡
The Network Effect — Charging Infrastructure & EV Adoption Spiral
EFE
EMEAFU

Even knowing the economics favour EVs, Ramesh hesitates — because of a coordination failure: consumers won't buy EVs until there are enough chargers, and investors won't build chargers until there are enough EVs. Individually rational, collectively terrible.

CFE
CHIOMA

Once a charging network reaches critical mass, we enter network effect territory — each new charger makes every existing EV more valuable, accelerating adoption non-linearly. The government's 12,000+ chargers and a target of one every 25 km on highways are designed to trigger that tipping point.

EFE
EMEAFU

Supply constraints also matter. Rare earth magnet shortages in mid-2025 disrupted Bajaj and TVS production — a supply-side shock from input scarcity. India's lithium and cobalt dependence on imports is why the ACC Battery Scheme invests ₹18,100 crore in domestic battery cells.

EV Adoption Flywheel — Network Effects & Market Equilibrium

Microeconomics Concept · Long-Run Equilibrium & Market Transition
The Road Ahead — When Will EVs Stand Alone?
🛣️
India's EV Journey — From Subsidy-Dependent to Self-Sustaining Market
CFE
CHIOMA

In microeconomics, long-run competitive equilibrium is reached when economic profits are zero, prices equal average total cost, and no firms want to enter or exit. The government's goal is getting India's EV market to this self-sustaining point — without perpetual subsidies.

EFE
EMEAFU

That is why FAME II's ₹15,000/kWh incentive became ₹10,000, then ₹5,000 — a planned subsidy phase-out. The test: can demand survive without the crutch? For two-wheelers: yes. For four-wheelers: not yet. The market is at different stages of the demand curve for different segments.

CFE
CHIOMA

India sold 2.27 million EVs in 2025 — crossing 2 million for the first time. The market is valued at USD 54 billion growing at 19% per year. Globally, India is now the world's largest market for electric three-wheelers, overtaking China. These are not subsidy-dependent numbers — they reflect genuine structural shift.

What Ramesh's Story Teaches Us About Markets

Closing Scene — Ramesh's Son Delivers Food on an EV, Pune 2025

CHIOMA

Let's bring it together. India's EV story is a masterclass in applied microeconomics. We saw demand curves shift right through subsidies. Supply curves shift right through PLI. A dominant firm evolve into a genuine oligopoly. And long-run equilibrium being pursued through gradual subsidy withdrawal.

EFE
EMEAFU

We saw market failures corrected via Pigouvian taxes. Coordination failures broken by public charger investment. Network effects creating self-reinforcing growth. And economies of scale driving battery costs down 90% — making EVs genuinely affordable for Ramesh.

CFE
CHIOMA

Ramesh's monthly fuel bill dropped from ₹4,500 to ₹900. His son delivers food on an EV. The air is cleaner. His savings have grown. This is microeconomics working — individual rational decisions, shaped by smart policy, creating collective good.

EFE
EMEAFU

The EV transition is not just environmental. It is deeply, fundamentally economic. And it all started with one man doing the maths at a petrol pump in Pune — and realising he could not afford to stay on petrol any longer.
